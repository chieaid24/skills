# Posts

Blog posts, announcements, release notes, changelogs, landing copy. The reader arrived from a link, owes you nothing, and leaves on the first sentence that wastes their time.

## The lede carries the claim

Open on the thing itself. Not the history that led to it, not the industry trend that frames it, not a definition of the problem space.

A post that spends three paragraphs establishing why the topic matters has already lost the reader who knew why it mattered - and failed to convince the one who did not, because the argument is still coming.

Put the sharpest true sentence first. Earn the second sentence with the first.

## One post, one idea

A post carries one claim. Supporting material either serves that claim or belongs in a different post.

The tell that you have two posts: a section you cannot cut without losing something, that also has nothing to do with the title.

## Show the thing

A working example, a real number, a before-and-after, a screenshot - any of these outperforms the paragraph describing it. Lead with the artifact, then say only what the artifact cannot show on its own.

This is also the cure for hype: `seamless` becomes a claim you have to prove, and the proof is more interesting than the adjective was.

## Release notes and changelogs

Group by what it means to the reader, not by what the commits did. The reader is scanning for one thing: does this affect me, and do I have to act?

- **Breaking** first, with the migration step. This is the only section some readers need.
- **New** next: what it does, one line, and where to look.
- **Fixed** last, and only what a user could have noticed. An internal refactor is not a release note.

Version numbers, dates, and links belong in the structure, not in the prose.

## Landing and marketing copy

The pull toward hype is strongest here and the cost is highest, because a stranger has no prior trust to spend on your adjectives.

Substitute mechanism for praise. `Runs your test suite on every keystroke` tells the reader what they get; `blazing-fast developer experience` tells them you have nothing to show.

Concrete nouns and verbs. The specific claim is also the more persuasive one.

## The reader is a stranger

Expand an acronym on first use. Drop ticket numbers, internal codenames, and team shorthand entirely. If a sentence only parses for someone who sat in the meeting, rewrite it or cut it.
